About

Hello, I am currently a PhD candidate at the Human Systems Laboratory, Department of Aeronautics and Astronautics, MIT. I work on tackling climate change with machine learning, together with Prof. Dava Newman, Cait Crawford, and Chris Hill. As deep learning algorithms are widely considered 'black-box', my work focuses on increasing trust and transparency when using deep learning in predicting and visualizing climate change. My research interests include physics-informed neural networks, uncertainty quantification, Earth system modeling, and forest carbon quantification. I have previously obtained my M.Sc. at MIT in Autonomous Systems, pioneering with Prof. Jon How safe and robust deep reinforcement learning techniques. If you are interested in working with us feel free to contact me and check out the projects below or two UROP position on fast deep learning-based climate models or visualizing the melting Antarctic.

Uncertainty-Aware Physics-Informed Neural Networks

Climate models are often running at low resolution (e.g., 100km), because resolving the subgrid processes (e.g., cloud formation, ocean eddies) with traditional numerical solver is very computionally expensive. Novel physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) promise to speed up numerical solvers by learning approximations of the subgrid processes, but traditionally do not express uncertainty. This preliminary work explores the use of uncertainty quantification in neural networks for physics-informed neural networks.

Digital Twin Earth: Coasts

Simulating coastal floods at high-resolution is computationally too expensive for real-time inference, uncertainty quantification, or low-income countres. We're leveraging neural operators, a new physics-infused deep learning technique to learn a coastal flood model that is magnitudes faster. Paper in progress.

Earth Intelligence Engine

As climate change increases the intensity of natural disasters, society needs better tools for climate risk communication. We are creating a "Google Earth of the future"; a global visualization of how climate change will shape our landscape. On our path, we proposed the first deep learning pipeline to ensure physical-consistency in synthetic satellite imagery and are publishing a dataset on 25k labelled satellite images of coastal floods and melting Arctic sea ice. Explore our results at trillium.tech/eie.

OneForest

Monitoring forest conservation projects, one of the cheapest carbon sinks, is expensive and inaccurate. We create deep learning-based forest inventories from drone imagery, collected by locals, to cheaply and accurately estimate the tree-sequestered carbon. The transparently estimated carbon certificates are sold to companies, aiming for carbon neutrality, and allow locals to generate a livelihood from maintaining rainforest and rebuilding rainforest.

The World as a Graph: Improving El Nino Forecasts with Graph Neural Networks

Deep learning-based models have recently outperformed state-of-the-art seasonal forecasting models, such as for predicting El Niñno-Southern Oscillation. However, current deep learning models are based on convolutional neural networks which are difficult to interpret and can fail to model large-scale atmospheric patterns. We propose the first application of graph neural networks to long range forecasting, because they can better capture spatially distant dependencies. We show that our model, Graphiño, outperforms state-of-the-art machine learning-based models for forecasts up to six months ahead and is more interpretable.

Certified Adversarial Robustness for Deep Reinforcement Learning

Deep Neural networks, used in commercially available driver assistance systems, can fail on hardly detectable adversaries, for example, white stickers on the road. This work has invented an add-on, real-time defense algorithm to certify the robustness of Deep Reinforcement Learning algorithms to such adversaries. Patent pending.

Safe Reinforcement Learning with Model Uncertainty Estimates

Deep neural networks can fail overconfidently on novel observations, for example, an uncollaborative pedestrian on a personal vehicle. This work pioneers a reinforcement learning framework that detects novel observations and cautiously avoids them by reasoning about the neural network's predictive confidence.
